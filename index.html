Abstract

Hieroglyphics, as one of the earliest writing systems, convey abstract meanings through symbolic and pictorial forms. While previous research has mainly focused on static hieroglyphic image generation and classification, the lack of dynamic and interpretable representations limits public engagement and understanding. To bridge this gap, we introduce the task of Controllable Hieroglyphics Animation Generation (CHAG), which aims to generate multi-character, trajectory-controllable hieroglyphic animations.
Despite the emergence of powerful Text-to-Video (T2V) and Image-to-Video (I2V) models, as well as various animation generation methods, directly applying them to symbolic glyphs remains challenging due to biases toward realism, structural instability, and lack of fine-grained motion control; moreover, none of these approaches are designed to preserve the symbolic integrity and motion semantics essential for hieroglyphic animation. To address these issues, we propose an end-to-end framework that transforms vector-based hieroglyphic inputs into expressive and coherent animations. Our approach extracts skeletons from glyphs to drive motion while preserving structural consistency through As-Rigid-As-Possible (ARAP) deformation. A differentiable video diffusion model enables local motion refinement guided by semantic prompts, while Bézier-curve-based trajectory modeling ensures precise global movement control. Furthermore, geometry-aware losses regularize skeleton structure throughout animation. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method achieves high-quality, structurally consistent hieroglyphic animations with controllable multi-part motions, highlighting its potential for cultural revitalization and educational applications.

How does it work?



Animation of hieroglyphic characters from static glyphs to dynamic animation, showcasing the preservation of structural integrity and motion through our method. The generated animations are guided by prompts and trajectories, demonstrating high-quality, interpretable character movement.
